About the Project

To showcase my growth in data visualization, I decided to revisit my first dashboard project and give it a revamp using the new skills I've acquired in data storytelling, design, and color theory.

While the data remains the same, I've created new and improved charts that address the limitations of the originals, such as enhanced clarity, deeper insights, and strategic use of color to highlight the key takeaways. I'm excited to share this journey and demonstrate how my skills have grown!

Introduction

Bellabeat is a high-tech health and wellness company which offers a variety of wellness products focusing on women's health. Their wearable devices which connect to the Bellabeat app track activity, sleep, and stress. Collecting data on activity, sleep, stress, and reproductive health has allowed Bellabeat to empower women with knowledge about their own health and habits.

About the Data

The Kaggle FitBit Fitness Tracker Data by MÖBIUS was used for this project. I was originally crowd sourced from 30 eligible Fitbit users between March 12 and June 12 2016 by: Furberg, Robert; Brinton, Julia; Keating, Michael; Ortiz, Alexa.​ It contains information about FitBit user activity, steps, sleep and expended calories.

I sourced Google Trends Search Term Data for step, sleep and activity tracking to uncover trends in the smart device market.

Insights & Chart Changes

The Google Trends Search Term Data shows that over time people have become less interested in activity tracking and much more interested in step and sleep tracking.

The original chart is very busy, difficult to read, and the axis text is cut off. The new chart highlights the most important information using text and color. Which let's the viewer quickly see the trends over time.

58% of the Fitbit participants included a moderate number of steps (5,000 - 10,000 steps/day), but only 21% reached the CDC an AHA recommended 10,000 steps per day. The remaining 21% were sedentary taking less than 5,000 steps per day.

The original chart contains unecessary elemants such as user id numbers, grid lines and too many colors which make it difficult to read. The new chart highlights the fact that only a small percentage of participants took the recommended steps.

The original chart contains too many colors and doesn't highlight the most important information. In the new chart one can quickly see that the participants took the most steps on Saturday and the least on Sunday. It also includes more context by showing the number of users and the amount of steps they took on average.

This chart wasn't included in the original dashboards. It clearly shows that participants were sedentary the majority of the time.

The original chart contains too many colors and doesn't highlight the information adequately. In the new chart one can see that Sunday was the day that participants had the most trouble falling asleep at 51 minutes. It also includes average time to fall asleep and average hours of sleep.

This new chart which shows that more activity leads to more calories burned.

Recommendations


Marketing should be geared towards step and sleep tracking as these have been gaining popularity in recent years.

Since Bellabeat utilizes digital marketing extensively, leverage social media campaigns to inspire and motivate followers by sharing content that showcases the benefits of step and sleep tracking:
  • Sunday Recharge: Post empowering messages on Sunday mornings. The data shows this is the day that users take the fewest steps, take longer to fall asleep, and sleep the most.
  • Daily Step Challenge: Focus on step tracking initiatives by helping followers set achievable step goals through the day to motivate them to stay active.
  • Sleep Smarter: Post sleep hygiene tips and facts to show how sleep tracking can help followers gain insights into their sleep patterns so they can get deeper, more restorative sleep.

Data Limitations

  • The FitBit data is not ROCCC, it is bad quality data (outdated, not from the original source, and incomplete).
  • Of the 33 participants only 24 participated in the sleep data and 8 in the weight data​ collection. All 33 did participate in the daily activity data collection.
  • The data is possibly biased due to the small sample size.​
  • It does not include gender, age, ethnicity. This limits how tailored the recommendations can be.
  • More complete data with a much larger sample set is needed in order to come to more accurate conclusion​s.
